## 3-Tier Observability Model

These gaps motivate a formal framework for thinking about evidence in personality analysis.

| Tier | Label | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Private / Internal | Motivations, sentiment, values — what drives what is done and how. Largely inaccessible to external observers. Requires subject self-report or guided introspection with the subject. Self-identification is evidence of, but not a guarantee of, match. |
| 2 | Low-discoverability, low-legibility public | Exists publicly but requires prior knowledge or context to locate and interpret. Seeks evidence that supports, balances, or fills in claims from Tier 1. This is the tier where most of the missing evidence above lives. |
| 3 | High-discoverability, high-legibility public | Standard web-findable material: publications, talks, social media, professional profiles. Effective for public figures, and especially effective when the subject has an authentic public persona — but persona and intrinsic self should not be assumed to be the same. |

**Key implication:** The initial analysis relied almost entirely on Tier 3 evidence. A complete case study requires deliberate effort at Tiers 1 and 2, ideally through subject collaboration or domain expert curation. This feedback session itself is a Tier 1 input.

## Open Questions

### 1. Color-Behavior Fingerprints

Are some color orientations structurally more likely to leave observable (discoverable, legible) public traces than others?

For example: Blue's systematic documentation tendency, White's institutional track record, and Green's community embeddedness may all generate Tier 3 evidence naturally. Red's ephemeral expressiveness and Black's strategic privacy may systematically produce *less* Tier 3 evidence relative to the underlying intensity of the orientation.

If so, an analysis that relies only on Tier 3 evidence will *underweight* Red and Black attributes — not because they are less present, but because they leave fewer discoverable fingerprints.

### 2. Persona vs. Intrinsic Self

Should the analysis explicitly distinguish *public persona* (how the subject presents publicly) from something more intrinsic (privately-held values, motivations, felt sense of identity)?

Three stances are possible:

- **Conflate them:** Justified if authenticity between public and private selves is established — but that claim itself requires Tier 1 and Tier 2 evidence to support, not just Tier 3.
- **Distinguish them explicitly:** Surfaces the gap between public-facing color signals and privately-held ones. Potentially more rigorous, but requires richer evidence to sustain.
- **Use persona as a stepping stone:** Treat public evidence as a starting hypothesis, then iteratively refine it with Tier 1 and Tier 2 data. Avoids the mistake of treating observable proxies as the underlying phenomenon — the evidence is not the thing being analyzed.

The third stance seems most defensible for a case study that is actively being curated with the subject's participation.
